## Neural and Learned Compression

For four decades, codec design was a craft: a human engineer hand-built a transform (the DCT, the wavelet), a quantizer, and an entropy coder, then tuned them against perceptual studies. The deep-learning era asked a different question. *What if we never specify the transform at all, and instead let gradient descent discover it directly from images, with the bitrate itself as part of the loss?* This is **end-to-end learned compression**, and since 2017 it has rewritten the research frontier.

### The core idea: an autoencoder with a price tag

Recall the classical pipeline: a *transform* decorrelates the signal, the result is *quantized* (rounded to a finite alphabet, the only lossy step), and an *entropy coder* writes the symbols to bits, using shorter codes for likelier symbols (its cost is the Shannon entropy of the symbol distribution). The learned version keeps this skeleton but makes every box a neural network.

An **autoencoder** is a pair of networks: an *encoder* $g_a$ that maps an image $x$ to a compact latent vector $y = g_a(x)$, and a *decoder* $g_s$ that reconstructs $\hat{x} = g_s(\hat{y})$. The latent is quantized to $\hat{y}$, then entropy-coded. The training objective is a **rate–distortion loss**, optimized directly:

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{R(\hat{y})}_{\text{bits}} + \lambda \underbrace{D(x, \hat{x})}_{\text{distortion}}.$$

Here $D$ is typically mean squared error and $R$ is the expected codelength, $-\log_2 p(\hat{y})$, under a *learned* probability model $p$. The Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$ slides you along the rate–distortion curve: large $\lambda$ buys quality, small $\lambda$ buys small files. Crucially, the entropy model $p$ is trained *jointly* with the encoder and decoder, so the network learns a representation that is *easy to compress*, not merely accurate.

### Ballé's breakthrough and the quantization trick

The foundational paper is **Johannes Ballé, Valero Laparra, and Eero Simoncelli, "End-to-end Optimized Image Compression," ICLR 2017** (with a 2016 precursor introducing *generalized divisive normalization*, a biologically inspired nonlinearity). It faced one nasty obstacle: **quantization (rounding) has zero gradient almost everywhere**, so backpropagation cannot flow through it. Their fix, now standard, is **quantization-as-noise**: during training, replace rounding $\lfloor y \rceil$ with adding uniform noise $y + \mathcal{U}(-\tfrac12, \tfrac12)$. This is a smooth, differentiable surrogate whose distribution matches the quantization error, and it lets the whole pipeline train by gradient descent. The connection runs deeper: the rate–distortion loss is exactly the objective of a **variational autoencoder** (Kingma & Welling, 2013), with the rate term playing the role of the KL divergence between the noisy latent and the prior. Compression *is* variational inference.

### The hyperprior: modeling the model

A fixed prior $p(\hat{y})$ is too rigid, because the right distribution for the latents varies across an image (smooth sky vs. busy foliage). In **"Variational Image Compression with a Scale Hyperprior," Ballé, Minnen, Singh, Hwang, Johnston (ICLR 2018)**, the authors added a second, smaller autoencoder on top. This **hyperprior** transmits a few extra "side-information" bits describing the *local statistics* (specifically the scale of a Gaussian) of the main latents — it compresses a description of the entropy model itself. The decoder reads the side information first, configures its Gaussian per-element, then decodes the main payload cheaply. This was the first learned codec to clearly beat the best hand-built ones on perceptual metrics.

**David Minnen, Johannes Ballé, and George Toderici (NeurIPS 2018)** then combined the hyperprior with an **autoregressive context model**, predicting each latent element from its already-decoded neighbors (the same trick PNG and modern codecs use spatially). Their joint model was the first learned method to beat **BPG** (the still-image codec derived from HEVC/H.265) on both PSNR and MS-SSIM, with a 15.8% size reduction over the prior learned art. The cost was severe and remains the field's central wound: autoregressive decoding is inherently *serial* — element $n$ needs element $n-1$ — making the decoder hundreds of times slower than a classical one.

### Generative codecs: optimizing perception, not pixels

MSE-trained codecs share a flaw inherited from rate–distortion theory: at very low bitrates they produce *blurry* reconstructions, because averaging over plausible details minimizes squared error. But blur is exactly what the human eye hates. The **rate–distortion–perception tradeoff** (Blau & Michaeli, 2019) formalized this: realism (matching the *distribution* of natural images) is a third axis, in tension with low distortion.

The landmark is **"High-Fidelity Generative Image Compression" (HiFiC), Mentzer, Toderici, Tschannen, Agustsson (NeurIPS 2020)**, which added a **GAN** (generative adversarial network) loss: a discriminator network is trained to tell reconstructions from real images, and the decoder is rewarded for fooling it. The result *hallucinates* plausible texture — hair, grass, brick — that is statistically right even if not pixel-identical. In human studies, HiFiC at one bitrate was preferred over MSE codecs using *twice* the bits. The honest caveat: these are no longer faithful reconstructions; they are *generated* images conditioned on a code, and they can invent details that were never there — a real hazard for medical or forensic use.

After 2022, **diffusion models** (which generate by iteratively denoising) supplanted GANs for perceptual quality. By 2025–2026, codecs such as **StableCodec (ICCV 2025)** and **OneDC (NeurIPS 2025)** piggyback on large pre-trained text-to-image diffusion models, reaching *ultra-low* bitrates below 0.01 bits per pixel by transmitting only a semantic sketch and letting the generative prior fill in the rest. Their problem was speed — multi-step denoising is glacial — so the 2025 frontier is **one-step distillation**, cutting reconstruction to a single network pass (roughly 20× faster) while preserving realism.

### Learned video and implicit representations

Video added temporal prediction. **DVC (Lu et al., CVPR 2019)** was the first fully end-to-end neural video codec, replacing every block of the classical hybrid coder (motion estimation, residual coding) with networks. The dominant line since is Microsoft's **DCVC series** (2021–2024), which discarded the old "predict-then-subtract residual" idea for **conditional coding**: the network uses temporal context as a *condition* on the entropy model rather than computing an explicit residual, which is information-theoretically tighter. **DCVC-DC/FM (2023–2024)** reportedly surpass the latest standard **VVC/H.266** in efficiency, though again at heavy compute cost.

A radically different idea is **implicit neural representation (INR)** compression. **COIN, "Compression with Implicit Neural Representations," Dupont et al. (ICLR 2021 workshop)** stores an image not as pixels or latents but as *the weights of a small network that has been overfit to that single image* — an MLP mapping pixel coordinate $(x,y)$ to color $(r,g,b)$. To decode, you simply evaluate the net at every coordinate. The "compression" is the smallness of the network plus quantization of its weights. COIN beat JPEG at low rates with no entropy coder at all. The descendants (NeRV for video, COIN++) are elegant and decode fast, but *encoding* means training a network per file — minutes to hours — so they remain niche.

### State of play, 2026: where learned codecs win and lose

The verdict is genuinely mixed. **Learned codecs win decisively on perceptual quality at low bitrate**, where generative reconstruction beats any block-transform codec, and they now match or exceed VVC on standard metrics for both images and video. They are also flexible: one architecture retargets to new domains by retraining.

They **lose on speed, hardware, and standardization**. Context-model decoders can be 50× slower than classical ones and lack the encode/decode asymmetry that makes streaming practical (HEVC/AV1 decode 100–1000× faster than they encode; neural codecs are roughly symmetric and slow on both ends). They demand GPUs/NPUs absent from most decoding hardware, raising energy concerns. And reproducibility is fragile — a bit-exact match between encoder and decoder requires identical floating-point arithmetic across platforms, a notorious headache.

Standardization is the bellwether: **JPEG AI** became the first international standard for learning-based image coding, with its first edition published in October 2024 and approved as an ISO/IEC/ITU International Standard in early 2025. It claims roughly 30% gains over VVC-intra while explicitly targeting *both* human viewing and machine vision (object detection on the compressed latent, without full decode). Whether neural codecs displace AV1/VVC in the wild now hinges less on rate–distortion curves and more on silicon, power budgets, and the slow grind of deployment.
