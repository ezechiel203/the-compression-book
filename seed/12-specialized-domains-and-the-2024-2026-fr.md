## Specialized Domains and the 2024-2026 Frontier

General-purpose compressors (gzip, zstd) treat their input as an opaque byte stream. But the biggest practical wins of the last decade have come from the opposite move: exploit *what you know about the data*. The slogan is "the best model of the data is the best compressor," and in specialized domains the modeler is a human who understands the physics, the schema, or the network architecture.

### Genomic compression: differences against a reference

A human genome is ~3.2 billion bases drawn from a 4-letter alphabet {A,C,G,T}. Naively that is ~2 bits/base, ~800 MB. But a sequencing experiment produces hundreds of millions of short, overlapping, error-laden *reads*, with per-base **quality scores** (confidence estimates) attached — raw FASTQ files run into hundreds of gigabytes. The key insight, formalized in the **CRAM** format (Markus Hsi-Yang Fritz et al., 2011; standardized by the GA4GH consortium), is **reference-based compression**: any two human genomes differ by only ~0.1%, so instead of storing each read, you align it to a shared reference genome and store only the *position* plus the handful of *mismatches* (substitutions, insertions, deletions). The residual — what's left after subtracting the prediction (the reference) — is tiny and is then entropy-coded. This is the same "predict-then-code-the-residual" pattern as video inter-frame prediction, applied to biology. CRAM 3.1 (2022) added better entropy back-ends; the quality scores, which are noisier and dominate the file size, are the hard part and are usually compressed lossily (binning confidence levels). Illumina's DRAGEN ORA productized this. The 2024-2026 frontier (e.g. JARVIS3, 2024; learning-based and LLM-assisted compressors like AgentGC, 2026) pushes context-mixing neural predictors for the reference-free case, but reference-based CRAM remains the deployed workhorse.

### Scientific floating-point compression: error-bounded lossy

HPC simulations (climate, fluid dynamics, cosmology) emit petabytes of double-precision arrays. IEEE-754 floats are nearly incompressible losslessly — the low mantissa bits are essentially noise. The breakthrough was admitting the data is already an approximation of reality and offering **error-bounded lossy** compression: the user sets a tolerance (absolute or relative), and the compressor guarantees every reconstructed value lies within it. Two lineages dominate. **zfp** (Peter Lindstrom, LLNL, 2014) partitions arrays into small blocks (4×4×4), applies an orthogonal-ish transform to decorrelate neighboring values (akin to a DCT), then bit-plane-codes coefficients most-significant-bit first, truncating once the error bound is met — and it supports random access into compressed arrays. **SZ** (Sheng Di & Franck Cappello, Argonne, 2016) instead *predicts* each value from its neighbors (curve-fitting / Lorenzo predictor), quantizes the residual against the error bound, and entropy-codes. As of 2024-2026 the action is on GPUs (cuSZ, **CUSZ-i** at SC24 with high-ratio multi-level interpolation) and on preserving *derived quantities* — guaranteeing not just pointwise error but that critical points, topology, or vector-field features survive, since scientists care about those, not raw values.

### Columnar and time-series: the analytics workhorse

Analytical databases store tables **column-by-column** rather than row-by-row, because a column holds values of one type with low entropy and high local redundancy. **Apache Parquet** (2013, from Google's Dremel design) and **ORC** layer cheap, composable codes: **dictionary encoding** (replace repeated strings by small integer IDs), **run-length encoding (RLE)** (store "value × count" for runs), **bit-packing** (if values fit in 5 bits, don't waste 32), and **delta encoding** (store successive differences) — then optionally a byte-stream compressor (Snappy/zstd) on top. Ratios of 5-10× are routine, 30×+ on sorted/low-cardinality columns. For **time-series**, Facebook's **Gorilla** (Pelkonen et al., VLDB 2015) is canonical: timestamps are regular, so **delta-of-delta** encoding makes most of them a single bit; consecutive float values are similar, so it XORs adjacent doubles and stores only the differing middle bits. This routinely hits >90% reduction on monitoring metrics and underpins systems like InfluxDB and Prometheus. Research compressors like **BtrBlocks** (2023) automate per-column scheme selection.

### Neural network model compression: a field of its own

A trained network is also "data," and shrinking it is now its own subfield, framed explicitly as compression. The foundational trio: **pruning** (Han, Mao & Dally, "Deep Compression," 2015) removes weights with small magnitude — a sparse network has lower description length; **knowledge distillation** (Hinton, Vinyals & Dean, 2015) trains a small *student* to mimic a large *teacher*'s soft output distribution, transferring information the hard labels don't carry; and **low-rank factorization** (and later **LoRA**, Hu et al., 2021) approximates a weight matrix $W \approx AB$ with $A,B$ thin — fewer parameters because the true rank is low.

The dominant lever today is **quantization**: store weights in 8, 4, or fewer bits instead of 16. The non-obvious finding (Dettmers; Frantar et al.) is that a small fraction of *outlier* weights/activations carry most of the loss, so the trick is mixed precision. **GPTQ** (Frantar, Ashkboos, Hoefler & Alistarh, 2022) quantizes weights one column at a time, using second-order (Hessian) information to compensate each rounding error in the still-unquantized weights — a one-shot, calibration-set method good to 4-bit/3-bit. **AWQ** (Lin et al., MIT, MLSys 2024 Best Paper) instead identifies *salient* weights by their activation magnitude and rescales to protect them. Going below 4 bits, vector-quantization methods (QuIP#, QTIP, VPTQ, 2024-25) quantize *groups* of weights jointly to exploit correlations, but accuracy still degrades sharply in the sub-2-bit regime — that limit is real, not yet solved (2026 entrants like D2Quant chip at it).

### LLM inference: KV-cache, context, and embeddings

At inference, the **KV-cache** — the stored key/value vectors for every past token — grows linearly with context and dominates memory for long sequences. Compressing it is the hottest 2024-2026 axis. Three families: **quantization** of cached K/V (often 4-bit, with rotation tricks like PolarQuant, AISTATS 2026, to tame outliers); **eviction** (drop low-attention tokens — H2O, SnapKV); and **architectural** low-rank compression baked in at training time. The landmark is **Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA)** in DeepSeek-V2 (May 2024) and V3 (Dec 2024): keys and values are jointly projected to a small shared latent and only that latent is cached, cutting KV memory ~93% versus MHA while *matching or beating* full attention quality — compression as a first-class architectural choice, not a post-hoc patch. Separately, **prompt/context compression** (LLMLingua, Microsoft 2023) drops or summarizes low-information tokens from the prompt itself.

Finally, **embedding/vector compression** for retrieval (RAG). Semantic search stores millions of high-dimensional vectors; **product quantization (PQ)** (Jégou, Douze & Schmid, 2011) splits each vector into sub-vectors and replaces each with the nearest of 256 learned centroids — a vector becomes a few bytes, with distances computed via lookup tables. OPQ adds a learned rotation first; **binary quantization** (1 bit/dimension, Hamming-distance search) gives ~32× compression while keeping ~80-95% recall and is now a default in FAISS, ScaNN, and vector databases.

### Hype vs. deployed (mid-2026)

Deployed and boring-but-essential: CRAM, Parquet/Gorilla, zfp/SZ, 4-bit LLM weight quantization, PQ/binary embeddings, MLA. Promising but still moving: sub-3-bit LLM quantization, aggressive KV-cache eviction (lossy in ways that bite on long-reasoning tasks), and LLM-based "learned" compressors for genomics/general data — these often win on ratio in papers but lose on speed, and pure-neural lossless compression remains too slow for production. The honest open problem across all these domains is the same one Shannon posed: the better your model, the better your compression — and we are now compressing by *building models of the data*, which means the frontier of compression has merged with the frontier of machine learning itself.
