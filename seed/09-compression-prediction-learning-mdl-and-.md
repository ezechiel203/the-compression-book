## Compression = Prediction = Learning: MDL and Universal Coding

Everything in this guide so far has treated compression as an engineering problem: pack the bits tighter. This section reveals the deeper truth that the rest of the field rests on. **To compress data well is to predict it well, and to predict it well is to have learned its structure.** These are not three analogous activities; up to a measurable gap they are *the same activity*. Once you internalize this, the leap from Huffman codes to large language models stops looking like a leap at all — it is one idea pursued with progressively better models.

### The one identity that fuses coding and probability

Recall the central fact from entropy coding (covered earlier): an ideal lossless code assigns a symbol of probability \(p\) a codeword of length \(-\log_2 p\) bits, and arithmetic coding achieves this for *any* assigned distribution, to within a fraction of a bit over a whole message. This makes a **probabilistic model a compressor and a compressor a probabilistic model**, mechanically interchangeable.

Now suppose the data is truly generated by a distribution \(P\), but your model believes it is \(Q\). Your expected code length per symbol is the **cross-entropy**

\[
H(P,Q) = -\mathbb{E}_{x\sim P}\big[\log_2 Q(x)\big] = H(P) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\,\|\,Q).
\]

Read this slowly, because it is the hinge of the whole field. The best you could ever do is \(H(P)\), the source's true entropy (Shannon's 1948 lower bound: no lossless code beats it). Every bit you spend *above* that floor is exactly the **Kullback–Leibler divergence** \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P\|Q)\) — a precise, non-negative measure of how wrong your model is. Your **wasted bits are your modeling error, in the same units.**

And here is the punchline for machine learning. The quantity a modern model minimizes during training — the **log loss** or **cross-entropy loss** \(-\log Q(x)\) on the next symbol — is *literally the code length* you would pay to transmit that symbol using the model. A language model trained to minimize next-token log loss is, with no reinterpretation, being trained to minimize the compressed size of its training corpus. "Predict the next symbol well" and "compress the stream well" are the identical objective, written in two notations.

### Universal coding: compressing without knowing the source

There is a catch the identity quietly assumes: it requires the *true* \(P\), which you never have. A real compressor must work on a source whose statistics are unknown in advance. This is the problem of **universal coding** — building a single code that performs nearly as well as the best code *in some class*, no matter which member of the class actually generated the data.

The trick is **adaptation**. Process the stream left to right; at each step predict the next symbol from what you have already seen; update. The excess cost over the ideal — the **redundancy** — provably shrinks as the message grows. Two foundational results from the 1970s–80s make this rigorous. Rissanen and Langdon's **arithmetic coding** (1976–1979) decoupled the *model* from the *coder* entirely, so any sequence of predicted distributions can be turned into near-optimal bits. And the **Krichevsky–Trofimov estimator** (1981) showed that for an unknown Bernoulli/multinomial source, gently smoothed counts (add-½ to each symbol) cost only about \(\tfrac{k}{2}\log_2 n\) extra bits over \(n\) symbols for \(k\) parameters. That \(\tfrac{1}{2}\log_2 n\) **per parameter** is the universal price of *learning* a parameter from data — a number that will reappear in a moment, and again decades later in statistics as the BIC penalty.

### Rissanen's MDL: Occam's razor with a bit-meter

In 1978, Jorma Rissanen, then at IBM, published **"Modeling by Shortest Data Description,"** coining the **Minimum Description Length** principle. His move was philosophical and operational at once. If a model lets you compress data, then the *total* description you must transmit to a receiver is two parts:

\[
L(\text{model}) + L(\text{data} \mid \text{model}).
\]

The first term is the cost of *stating the hypothesis*; the second is the cost of the *residual* — the data's surprises once the model explains the regularities. **MDL says: choose the model that minimizes this total.** This is **Occam's razor made quantitative.** A model too simple leaves a large residual (it under-fits, the second term blows up). A model too complex costs many bits to specify and merely memorizes noise (it over-fits, the first term blows up). The minimum sits at the genuine **regularity** in the data — and crucially, it requires *no* arbitrary regularization constant, no held-out validation set, no prior pulled from thin air. The bit is the universal currency, and overfitting becomes self-penalizing: noise, being incompressible, simply cannot be encoded short. Rissanen, inspired by Solomonoff, Kolmogorov, and Akaike but developing it independently, extended this through the 1980s into refined "stochastic complexity" and normalized-maximum-likelihood codes. The crude two-part form already recovers the \(\tfrac{1}{2}\log n\) per-parameter penalty from universal coding — the same number twice, because it is the same idea twice.

### Solomonoff: the ideal predictor that compresses everything

Push this to its logical extreme and you arrive at the most beautiful — and most uncomputable — object in the theory. Between 1960 and 1964, **Ray Solomonoff** asked: what is the *best possible* prior over all data sequences? His answer, **algorithmic probability**, assigns to a string a probability that aggregates over *every program* on a universal Turing machine that outputs it, weighting each program by \(2^{-(\text{its length})}\). Short programs dominate — Occam's razor, derived rather than assumed. The negative log of this quantity is essentially the **Kolmogorov complexity** \(K(x)\) (Kolmogorov 1965, Chaitin 1966, discovered independently): the length of the shortest program that prints \(x\). \(K(x)\) is the *true, model-free, ultimate compressed size* of an object.

**Solomonoff induction** — predict the next symbol by Bayesian mixing over all programs — is, in a precise theorem, the optimal universal predictor: its cumulative prediction error is bounded by the Kolmogorov complexity of the actual data-generating process. It is the perfect compressor and the perfect learner, unified. The honest catch: \(K(x)\) is **uncomputable** (no algorithm computes it in general, a corollary of the halting problem). Solomonoff induction is therefore an *ideal*, not a recipe — a north star, not a destination. Every practical compressor and every learning algorithm is a **computable approximation** to this incomputable optimum, trading universality for tractability.

### Why this is the hinge of the modern era

This equivalence is not folklore — it is now an explicit research program and even a *benchmark*. The **Hutter Prize** (launched 2006 by Marcus Hutter; since 2020 targeting `enwik9`, a 1 GB Wikipedia excerpt) pays for compressing human knowledge precisely *because* Hutter argues compression and intelligence are one; the record stood at ~110.8 MB (fx2-cmix, Knoll & Orav, accepted October 2024). And in 2023, Google DeepMind's **"Language Modeling Is Compression"** (Delétang, Ruoss, Veness, Hutter et al.; ICLR 2024) closed the loop empirically: pairing a 70B-parameter **Chinchilla** language model with arithmetic coding, they compressed LibriSpeech audio to **16.4%** of its raw size, crushing the specialist audio codec FLAC (**30.3%**) — using a model that had never seen audio, only the universal pressure to predict the next byte. The cost is brutal and honest: such "compressors" are 10–100× slower and heavier than classical codecs, and the model's own parameters dwarf the file unless amortized over a huge corpus.

But the conceptual verdict is settled. A neural network minimizing next-token log loss is a universal coder hunting for the source's true distribution; the loss curve *is* a compression curve.
