## Statistical Context Modeling: PPM, Context Mixing, PAQ, and the Hutter Prize

Every lossless compressor is, at heart, a probabilistic predictor wearing a coding jacket. Shannon's source coding theorem tells us that a symbol whose probability is `p` costs about `-log2(p)` bits to encode, and an *arithmetic coder* (see the entropy-coding section) can realize that ideal length to within a fraction of a bit. So the whole game reduces to one question: **how good is your model of `P(next symbol | everything seen so far)`?** The family of techniques covered here pushes that question to its extreme, accepting enormous computational cost in exchange for the best compression ratios humanity knows how to achieve. This is the *high-ratio frontier*, and it is where compression quietly merges with artificial intelligence.

### PPM: backing off through context lengths

The foundational idea is **PPM — Prediction by Partial Matching — introduced by John Cleary and Ian Witten in 1984** ("Data Compression Using Adaptive Coding and Partial String Matching," IEEE Trans. Communications). A *context* is simply the string of recent symbols; an *order-k* model predicts the next character from the previous `k` characters. High-order contexts are sharply predictive ("compressio**n**" almost certainly follows "compressio") but are seen rarely, so their statistics are sparse and unreliable. Low-order contexts are reliable but vague.

PPM's insight is to **blend orders by escaping**. It starts at the highest order it has data for, and if the upcoming symbol has never been seen in that context, it emits a special *escape* symbol and "backs off" to the next shorter context, repeating until the symbol is found (or it falls through to an order-(-1) uniform model that guarantees every symbol has nonzero probability). The deep, unanswered question is how much probability mass to reserve for that escape — the **zero-frequency problem** of assigning probability to events not yet observed. Different answers gave the variants PPMA and PPMB (Cleary & Witten, 1984), PPMC and PPMD (Alistair Moffat, 1990), and ultimately **PPMII** by **Dmitry Shkarin (2002)**, whose **PPMd** implementation reached roughly 2 bits per character on English text and became the best Calgary-corpus compressor of its day. PPMd is not academic trivia: it ships inside **7-Zip** and **WinRAR** to this day as a high-ratio text mode. PPM's weakness is structural — it commits to *one* context (the longest match) and merely backs off; it cannot, say, simultaneously weigh word-level and character-level evidence.

### Context mixing: many predictors, one verdict

**Context mixing (CM)** removes that restriction. Instead of picking a single context and escaping, you run **many models in parallel** — order-0 through order-N character contexts, whole-word models, sparse models that skip positions, models keyed on indentation or on the matching bracket — and you **combine all their predictions** into one probability for each bit. (CM compressors operate bit-by-bit: each model outputs `P(next bit = 1)`, which keeps the mixing math one-dimensional and cheap.)

How do you combine them? Early work used a weighted linear average. The decisive improvement was **logistic mixing**: map each model's probability `p_i` into the log-odds domain, `s_i = ln(p_i / (1 - p_i))`, take a weighted sum `S = Σ w_i s_i`, and squash back with the logistic function `P = 1/(1 + e^{-S})`. This is exactly a single-layer neural network — a *gated linear network* — and the weights are trained online by gradient descent to minimize coding cost (cross-entropy loss). Because coding cost *is* the loss, the compressor literally learns, byte by byte, which models to trust in which situations. A further trick, **SSE (Secondary Symbol Estimation)** or APM, takes the mixed probability and refines it through a learned calibration table — correcting the mixer's systematic over- and under-confidence.

### PAQ, ZPAQ, and cmix: the arms race

**Matt Mahoney built the PAQ family starting with PAQ1 in 2002**, growing directly out of his 2000 paper "Fast Text Compression with Neural Networks." PAQ became an open, collaborative arms race: dozens of contributors (Alexander Rhatushnyak, Przemysław Skibiński, Serge Osnach, and many others) added models and preprocessing through versions PAQ6, PAQ7 (which introduced logistic neural mixing), PAQ8, and specialized branches like the `paq8hp*` line tuned for Wikipedia. Mahoney later distilled the lessons into **ZPAQ (2009–2015)**, whose contribution was a *standardized, archival* container: the decompressor's model is described in a bytecode embedded in the archive, so a single fixed `zpaq` program can decompress files made by future, smarter compressors — solving the reproducibility headache that plagued PAQ. The current ratio champion is **cmix**, begun by **Byron Knoll around 2014**, which throws an even larger model ensemble (including an LSTM neural predictor) at the data and routinely tops the leaderboards.

### The Large Text Compression Benchmark and the Hutter Prize

To measure all this, Mahoney created the **Large Text Compression Benchmark**, built on **enwik8** (the first 100 MB of an English Wikipedia dump) and **enwik9** (1 GB). Wikipedia is deliberate: compressing it well demands modeling syntax, semantics, factual structure, and markup — a stand-in for general knowledge.

In **August 2006, Marcus Hutter founded the Hutter Prize** ("Compressing Human Knowledge"), originally on enwik8, expanded to **enwik9 with a 500,000 € purse in February 2020**. The rule is elegant: beat the standing record by `x%` and collect `x%` of the fund (5,000 € per percent), provided your self-extracting decompressor runs in **under ~100 hours on a single CPU core, within 10 GB RAM, no GPU**, with source released under a free license. Those constraints are the whole point — they forbid burying a giant pretrained model or compute farm and force the *algorithm* to carry the intelligence.

The winners trace the field's evolution: Mahoney's `paq8f` set the opening enwik8 baseline (2006); **Alexander Rhatushnyak won repeatedly with `paq8hp*` and later `phda9`** (2006–2017); on enwik9, Artemiy Margaritov's `starlit` (2021), Saurabh Kumar's `fast-cmix` (2023), and **Kaido Orav's `fx-cmix` (Feb 2024, 112,578,322 bytes)** advanced the mark. The current record is **`fx2-cmix` by Kaido Orav and Byron Knoll — 110,793,128 bytes, set 3 September 2024, awarded 7,950 €** — all cmix descendants. As of mid-2026 no smaller verified enwik9 entry has been awarded, and the practical reality is stark: these programs run on the order of **a day or more for a single gigabyte**, hundreds to thousands of times slower than `gzip`, with gigabytes of RAM — utterly unusable as general-purpose tools.

### Why "compression is intelligence" is taken seriously

The reason serious researchers stake a 500,000 € prize on this rests on **algorithmic information theory**. The *Kolmogorov complexity* of a string is the length of the shortest program that outputs it — the ultimate, uncomputable compression limit. **Solomonoff induction** (Ray Solomonoff, 1964) defines the optimal universal predictor by weighting every hypothesis by `2^{-(program length)}`, and one can prove it is essentially equivalent to optimal compression: **the better you compress, the better you predict, and prediction is the core of intelligence.** Hutter formalized this into **AIXI**, a (also uncomputable) model of optimal agency, and with Shane Legg defined a universal *intelligence measure* (2007). To predict the next Wikipedia character well, you must in effect *know things* — grammar, geography, arithmetic, that "Paris is the capital of" is followed by "France." The Hutter Prize is thus a wager that squeezing enwik9 is a clean, cheat-resistant proxy for machine knowledge.

That wager looks prescient: in 2023, DeepMind's "Language Modeling Is Compression" showed large language models are state-of-the-art compressors when paired with arithmetic coding — the same prediction-equals-compression duality, now at the scale of billions of parameters. The CM crowd and the LLM crowd are, mathematically, playing the identical game; the Hutter Prize simply insists you win it without a supercomputer.
