## Entropy Coding: Huffman, Arithmetic Coding, Range Coding, and ANS

An **entropy coder** is the component that turns a probability model into actual bits. This is the heart of compression's division of labor. A compressor is almost always split into two halves: a **model**, which estimates a probability distribution `P` over the next symbol given context, and a **coder**, which writes the symbol to the bitstream using a number of bits as close as possible to `-log2 P(symbol)`. Shannon's source coding theorem (1948) fixes the target: a source with entropy `H = -Σ P(s) log2 P(s)` bits/symbol cannot be compressed below `H` on average, and *can* be compressed arbitrarily close to it. Entropy coders are the machines that try to hit that bound. Everything else in a codec — dictionaries, transforms, predictors — exists to make the model's `P` sharper so that the entropy coder has less work and fewer bits to emit.

The reason this split matters: a symbol you believe occurs with probability `1/2` *deserves* exactly 1 bit; a symbol of probability `1/256` deserves 8 bits; a near-certain symbol of probability `0.99` deserves only about `0.0145` bits. The entire game is paying the fractional-bit cost the model dictates.

### Shannon–Fano and Huffman: prefix codes and their integer-bit tax

The first practical coders assigned each symbol its own codeword, a fixed bit string, such that no codeword is a prefix of another. This **prefix-free** (or **instantaneous**) property lets a decoder parse the stream left-to-right with no ambiguity: as soon as the bits read match a codeword, you emit that symbol and continue. **Shannon–Fano coding** (Claude Shannon and Robert Fano, ~1948–49) built such codes top-down by recursively splitting the symbol set into roughly equal-probability halves. It was good but not optimal.

In 1952, **David Huffman**, then a graduate student at MIT in Fano's class, found the optimal construction — and was reportedly trying to avoid the term paper that asked for it. His paper, *A Method for the Construction of Minimum-Redundancy Codes* (Proceedings of the IRE, September 1952), gives a bottom-up greedy algorithm: repeatedly merge the two least-probable symbols into a combined node, building a binary tree whose leaves are the symbols; the path from root to leaf spells the codeword. **Huffman coding is provably optimal among all prefix codes** — no other assignment of integer-length codewords achieves a shorter average length.

But "optimal among prefix codes" hides the catch: a prefix code *must* spend a whole number of bits per symbol. If your model says a symbol deserves `0.0145` bits, Huffman must round up to 1 — a 69× overpayment on that symbol. For skewed distributions, or when one symbol dominates (think a near-certain "0" in a sparse bitmap), Huffman bleeds badly: its average length can exceed entropy `H` by up to nearly 1 bit/symbol in the worst case, and stays stubbornly above `H` whenever probabilities aren't powers of `1/2`. This **integer-bits weakness** is the original sin Huffman cannot escape, and it motivated everything that followed.

### Arithmetic coding: the whole message as one interval

The fix, conceptually elegant, is to stop assigning bits to individual symbols and instead encode the *entire message* as a single number. **Arithmetic coding** represents a message as a subinterval of `[0, 1)`. You start with the full interval; each symbol narrows it by a factor equal to its probability, partitioning the current interval into subintervals sized by `P`. After processing the whole message you emit just enough bits to specify a point inside the final tiny interval. Because an interval of width `w` needs only about `-log2 w` bits to pin down, and `w` is the product of all the symbol probabilities, the total cost is `Σ -log2 P(s)` — *exactly the entropy*, with no per-symbol rounding. Fractional bits are accumulated and amortized across the message.

The idea traces to **Peter Elias** (~early 1960s, unpublished, requiring impractical infinite-precision arithmetic). The breakthrough was making it work in finite precision: **Jorma Rissanen** and, independently, **Richard Pasco** both published practical finite-precision schemes in 1976. Rissanen worked at **IBM**, and with **Glen Langdon** published the influential *Arithmetic Coding* (IBM Journal of Research and Development, 1979). Through renormalization — rescaling the interval and shipping out settled high-order bits as you go — arithmetic coding runs in bounded memory while staying within a tiny constant of the entropy bound.

Arithmetic coding's superiority (often ~5–10% smaller than Huffman, far more on skewed sources) came with two costs. First, **speed**: it needs multiplications and divisions per symbol, slow on the hardware of the era. Second, and more damaging historically, a **patent thicket**. IBM and others filed numerous arithmetic-coding patents (e.g., US 4,286,256; the **Q-coder**, refined with Mitsubishi into the **QM-coder**, US 4,905,297, filed 1988). The JPEG standard (1992) actually *specified* an arithmetic-coding mode roughly 10% better than its Huffman mode — but the Independent JPEG Group's free 1991 implementation shipped only Huffman, and chronic uncertainty over licensing meant virtually no JPEG decoder ever supported arithmetic coding. The patents made the better algorithm commercially radioactive. Most lapsed by ~2007 (US 4,905,297 in February 2007), but by then the de facto ecosystem had calcified around Huffman.

### Range coding: the same idea, side-stepping patents

**Range coding** is essentially arithmetic coding viewed as operating on an integer range with digits in an arbitrary base (often a byte at a time rather than a bit), trading a sliver of efficiency for speed and simpler renormalization. **G. Nigel N. Martin** described it at the Video and Data Recording Conference in Southampton, July 1979 — independently rediscovering Pasco's FIFO arithmetic code. Because Martin's formulation predated and sat outside the core IBM binary-coder patents, range coding became the pragmatic, believed-unencumbered way to get near-entropy coding. It powered codecs like LZMA (7-Zip) and many others through the 2000s.

### ANS: arithmetic-grade compression at Huffman speed

The modern revolution is **Asymmetric Numeral Systems (ANS)**, introduced by **Jarosław (Jarek) Duda** (Polish computer scientist, Jagiellonian University) — first arXiv'd in 2009 (arXiv:0902.0271), with the family refined through ~2013. ANS encodes the whole message into a single large natural number `x`, the *state*. Encoding a symbol `s` transforms `x` into a new, larger state via a rule that grows `x` by roughly `1/P(s)` — so rare symbols grow the state more (cost more bits) and common symbols barely grow it. The genius is that this transform is reversible and, in the **table-based variant (tANS)**, can be precomputed into a small finite-state-machine lookup table: encoding/decoding becomes a table lookup, a shift, and a comparison — *no multiplications or divisions per symbol*. There is also a **range variant (rANS)** that handles adaptive probabilities. One quirk: ANS naturally operates **LIFO** (last-in, first-out), so it encodes and decodes in opposite order, which the implementation must arrange.

The result was startling: **ANS achieves arithmetic coding's compression ratio at roughly Huffman's speed** — the long-standing trade-off collapsed. Adoption was swift and broad. **Facebook's Zstandard (zstd)** and **Apple's LZFSE** both shipped in 2015 using ANS for entropy coding; **JPEG XL** (the JPEG committee's 2017-onward image format, building on Google's PIK/2019) uses ANS; and it appears across Google, Microsoft, Dropbox, and Pixar pipelines.

Duda deliberately placed ANS in the public domain. That generosity triggered drama: **Google** filed a patent application broadly covering ANS use; after Duda's complaints, the USPTO issued a non-final rejection of all claims, and following EFF advocacy Google abandoned the application around 2018. Then in January 2022 **Microsoft** obtained a US patent on an rANS modification despite the prior art — a recurrence of exactly the patent-thicket dynamic that had once strangled arithmetic coding. As of mid-2026, ANS remains the dominant entropy coder in new general-purpose and image codecs, with research (e.g., 2024–2025 work on optimal tANS table construction) still tightening its already small gap to the entropy limit.
