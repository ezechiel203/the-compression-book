Lossless and Speech Coding

There is a big difference between a recording and a performance. A recording tries to capture the air pressure itself. A vocoder captures the idea of what was said, and reconstructs the air pressure from scratch.
— Loosely paraphrased from Manfred Schroeder, inventor of CELP, c. 1985

Here is a puzzle that will sharpen the lesson of the entire Volume III arc. Suppose you hand an audio compressor two recordings: the first is a full orchestral symphony — rich harmonics, dynamic drums, soaring strings. The second is a phone call recorded in a quiet room, one person talking, nothing else. The compressor using MP3 or AAC (Chapters 47 and 48) will squeeze both dramatically — ten-to-one or better — by exploiting what the human ear cannot hear. But now give the same files to a lossless compressor, and something strange happens: the symphony ends up about half its original size, while the phone call ends up at less than one-tenth. Why the colossal difference?

The answer is that lossless compression can only remove statistical redundancy — patterns that repeat in the numbers — whereas lossy perceptual coding removes biological redundancy, the sounds your ear would have ignored anyway. Music is rich, noisy, and statistically complex; the lossless coder cannot do much. Speech, by contrast, is generated by a surprisingly simple mechanical process — air blown through a pipe with resonant chambers — and that process has deep, exploitable structure. A codec that models the source of speech instead of the waveform itself can achieve extraordinary compression. That is what this chapter is about.

We will cover two distinct families. First, the lossless audio world: FLAC, ALAC, WavPack, and Monkey’s Audio — what they share, how they exploit short-term linear structure in audio samples, and why 50–60 % reduction is about as good as lossless gets for music. Second, the speech coding world: LPC-10, CELP, ACELP, AMR, and EVS — codecs that discard far more than any perceptual model would ever dare, because they know exactly what the human vocal tract can and cannot produce.

Where we are Chapters 46 and 47 taught us psychoacoustics and MP3: the idea of discarding signal energy that falls below the masking threshold. Chapters 48 and 49 added AAC and Opus. Before that, Chapters 37–39 covered the DCT and quantization: controlled rounding that trades quality for bits. Chapter 18 gave us Shannon entropy — the hard floor on lossless size — and Chapter 19 proved no lossless code can beat it. Chapter 25 built the integer and universal codes (unary, Elias, Golomb–Rice) we will reuse for residuals here, and Chapter 35 introduced the Burrows–Wheeler Transform, showing that even lossless coders benefit from transforming data into a friendlier shape before entropy coding. Now we apply the same “transform first, then code” philosophy to audio — first for the goal of perfect reconstruction, then for the goal of intelligible speech at near-impossibly-low bitrates.

By the end of this chapter you will be able to:

  • Explain why lossless audio compression achieves only 40–60 % reduction on music, and contrast that with the 90 %+ achievable by perceptual codecs.
  • Describe linear predictive coding (LPC): what it predicts, what the residual is, and why Rice/Golomb codes are near-optimal for encoding that residual.
  • Name the four major lossless audio codecs (FLAC, ALAC, WavPack, Monkey’s Audio), state who created each, its licence, and its distinctive design choice.
  • Define the vocoder concept: what is the source model, what are the parameters, and how do they reconstruct speech at the decoder?
  • Trace the evolution from LPC-10 (2400 bps) through CELP (1985) to ACELP, AMR-NB, AMR-WB, and EVS, naming key people and dates.
  • Explain what “algebraic codebook” means in ACELP and why it made the algorithm fast enough to run on a mobile phone.

Why Lossless Has a Ceiling

Before we meet any specific codec, we need to understand the ceiling. In Chapter 18 we learned that Shannon entropy sets a hard lower bound on how many bits any lossless compression scheme can use per symbol, and Chapter 19 proved that bound is unbeatable. Averaged over a long piece of music, that bound is stubbornly high. Why?

Recall from Chapter 18 that entropy is high when the probability distribution over symbols is spread out — when the next value is hard to predict. A CD-quality audio sample is a 16-bit integer in the range −32,768 to 32,767. Silence has very low entropy — it is just zeros. But a crash cymbal, a piano chord, or a plucked guitar string produces sample values that jump erratically over thousands of different values from moment to moment. Generic lossless compression — Huffman codes or arithmetic coding — cannot gain much traction.

The clever move, used by every lossless audio codec, is to avoid compressing the raw samples at all. Instead:

  1. Predict each sample from its recent neighbours using a mathematical formula.
  2. Compute the residual — the difference between the prediction and the actual sample.
  3. Entropy-code the residual.

The residual is much smaller in magnitude than the original sample. Where a sample might range ±30,000, the residual — the part the predictor got wrong — might typically range only ±200. A small-magnitude distribution is far more compressible.

Key idea Lossless audio coding = linear prediction (to produce small residuals) + entropy coding (to pack the residuals compactly). The predictor is tuned to audio; the entropy coder is often Rice/Golomb because the residuals follow a Laplacian-like distribution that makes those codes near-optimal.

Even so, the ceiling is real. The residuals of a complex musical passage are still statistically diverse — they are not all near zero — and their entropy cannot be made arbitrarily small without discarding information. For typical 44.1 kHz 16-bit stereo music, lossless codecs reduce files to 50–70 % of raw PCM size. The remaining 30–50 % can only be removed by throwing something away.

Linear Predictive Coding (LPC) — the Shared Engine

Every codec in this chapter — both lossless audio and speech — is built around the same core idea: linear predictive coding, or LPC.

The Basic Idea

Suppose you have a sequence of audio samples: 𝑥1,𝑥2,𝑥3,…. The sample at time 𝑛 is 𝑥𝑛. A linear predictor of order 𝑝 estimates 𝑥𝑛 as a weighted sum of the 𝑝 previous samples:

𝑥̂𝑛=𝑎1𝑥𝑛−1+𝑎2𝑥𝑛−2+…+𝑎𝑝𝑥𝑛−𝑝

The weights 𝑎1,𝑎2,…,𝑎𝑝 are called predictor coefficients. The error — the difference between prediction and reality — is the residual:

𝑒𝑛=𝑥𝑛−𝑥̂𝑛

The goal is to choose the coefficients so that the residuals 𝑒𝑛 are as small as possible, in the mean-squared-error sense.

Recall. Matrices and vectors are from Chapter 12; 𝐸[⋅], the expectation (a probability-weighted average), is from Chapter 10. A matrix equation 𝑅𝒂=𝒓 is just a compact way to write 𝑝 ordinary linear equations in the 𝑝 unknowns 𝑎1…𝑎𝑝.

A linear predictor finds coefficients 𝑎1…𝑎𝑝 that minimise the mean squared residual 𝐸[𝑒𝑛2] — the average of the squared error, the spread idea from Chapter 10. Setting the derivative with respect to each coefficient to zero (the slope-is-zero condition for a minimum, from Chapter 11) leads to the Yule-Walker equations:

𝑅𝒂=𝒓

where 𝑅 is the 𝑝×𝑝 autocorrelation matrix with entries 𝑅𝑖𝑗=𝐸[𝑥𝑛𝑥𝑛−|𝑖−𝑗|] — a number that measures how much a sample resembles one |𝑖−𝑗| steps away — and 𝒓 is the vector of autocorrelations [𝑅(1),𝑅(2),…,𝑅(𝑝)]. Solving this system gives the least-squares optimal predictor coefficients. In practice the Levinson-Durbin algorithm (1947/1960) solves it in 𝑂(𝑝2) rather than the 𝑂(𝑝3) of general Gaussian elimination, by exploiting the Toeplitz (shift-invariant) structure of 𝑅 — every diagonal of 𝑅 holds the same value.

Tiny example. Suppose three consecutive samples are [100,108,113]. A first-order predictor uses 𝑥̂3=𝑎1×108. If we set 𝑎1=113108≈1.046, the residual is zero for this pair. Over many samples the optimal 𝑎1 for slowly-changing audio is close to 1 — the previous sample is a good predictor of the next.

For audio, a predictor of order 4–32 typically suffices. FLAC uses up to order 32; mobile speech codecs such as AMR use order 8–10 for narrowband or up to 16 for wideband. The coefficients are computed on a short block (frame) of samples — typically 10–40 ms — because audio statistics change slowly on that timescale but shift significantly over longer spans.

The Residual’s Distribution

The residuals of a well-tuned LPC predictor follow an approximately Laplacian distribution: a sharp peak near zero, with tails that fall off exponentially. This is different from a Gaussian bell curve (which falls off faster) or a uniform distribution (which has flat tails). The Laplacian shape is important because it means most residuals are small, but occasional large values occur. Rice and Golomb codes are designed precisely for this shape.

∑ Go Further · The Maths — Laplacian, Gaussian, and Uniform — Three Distribution Shapesskip if familiar

A distribution (Chapter 10) just says how likely each value is. Three shapes recur throughout compression:

  • Uniform: every value in a range is equally likely — a flat line. A fair die is uniform. Hard to compress; nothing to predict.
  • Gaussian (the “bell curve”): values cluster around an average, and the probability drops off as 𝑒−𝑥2 — very fast, so extreme values are rare.
  • Laplacian (a “two-sided exponential”): also peaked at zero, but it drops off only as 𝑒−|𝑥|. That slower decay means a sharper central spike and fatter tails than a Gaussian: the typical value is tiny, yet the odd big spike is not shocking.

Why it matters here. Prediction error in audio, images, and speech is almost always Laplacian-shaped — a flood of near-zero values with rare large ones. A Rice/Golomb code spends very few bits on the common small values and accepts a long codeword for the rare big ones, which is exactly the right trade for this shape. (It is the same shape JPEG’s quantized DCT coefficients took in Chapter 42.)

We built Golomb and Rice codes from scratch in Chapter 25 (Integer and Universal Codes) and used them again in Chapter 35 for BWT residuals. A Golomb code with parameter 𝑀 encodes an integer 𝑛≥0 as:

  • Quotient 𝑞=⌊𝑛/𝑀⌋ encoded in unary (that many 1-bits, then a 0).
  • Remainder 𝑟=𝑛mod𝑀 encoded in binary (using ⌈log2𝑀⌉ bits).

A Rice code is the special case 𝑀=2𝑘 (a power of two), which makes the remainder just the last 𝑘 bits of 𝑛 — no division needed, only bit shifts. For signed residuals, map them to non-negative integers first: 0,1,−1,2,−2,… (called zigzag or interleaving).

Why optimal for Laplacian? For a geometric distribution (discrete analogue of exponential/Laplacian), the optimal entropy code length for value 𝑛 is approximately 𝑛𝑀+log2𝑀 bits, which is exactly what a Golomb code gives. For each partition of samples, FLAC picks the 𝑘 that minimises total coded length — a one-dimensional search.

Recall. Entropy from Chapter 18: 𝐻=−∑𝑖𝑝𝑖log2𝑝𝑖 bits per symbol — the average surprise, and the lossless floor.

Encoding and Decoding with LPC

Encoding is straightforward:

  1. Divide the audio stream into frames (FLAC uses 4,096–65,536 samples).
  2. Within each frame, choose a predictor order 𝑝 and solve for coefficients 𝑎1…𝑎𝑝.
  3. Quantize the coefficients slightly (they must be stored in the file, costing bits).
  4. Compute all residuals 𝑒𝑛.
  5. Entropy-code the residuals with Rice codes.
  6. Write coefficients + coded residuals to the bitstream.

Decoding reverses the process: decode the coefficients and residuals, then reconstruct each sample from the predictor formula:

𝑥𝑛=𝑥̂𝑛+𝑒𝑛=𝑎1𝑥𝑛−1+…+𝑎𝑝𝑥𝑛−𝑝+𝑒𝑛

Since the decoder reconstructs exactly the same sequence of integers, the process is perfectly lossless — every bit of the original file is recovered.

FLAC — the Open Standard

FLAC — Free Lossless Audio Codec — is the dominant lossless audio format. It was created by Josh Coalson and first released on January 15, 2001. In January 2003, it was adopted under the Xiph.Org Foundation umbrella. In December 2024, after years of work by the IETF CELLAR working group, FLAC was formally specified as RFC 9639 — giving it the same rigorous standards-track status as HTTP or TLS.

The licence is intentionally permissive: the format specification and reference software are under a BSD licence, and there are no patents to license. This matters enormously. The entire lossless audio ecosystem could adopt FLAC without legal risk, and hardware manufacturers — portable players, network streamers, smartphones — could implement it freely. Today FLAC is the default lossless format for music distribution on Bandcamp, Qobuz, and numerous archival services.

How FLAC Works

FLAC divides the audio into blocks (also called frames) of 4,096 to 65,536 samples. Within each block it applies one of several predictor types:

  • Verbatim: no prediction; store raw samples (used for the first few frames, or when prediction would not help).
  • Constant: all samples are the same value — store just one number.
  • Fixed prediction: one of four simple polynomial predictors (order 0–3) computed without any analysis, requiring no per-frame coefficient storage.
  • LPC prediction: full least-squares LPC up to order 32, with quantized coefficients stored in the frame header.

FLAC chooses whichever predictor yields the smallest total cost (coefficients + Rice-coded residuals). For most music, order-6 to order-12 LPC wins. The Rice parameter 𝑘 is optimized per partition of residuals within each frame.

FLAC also stores MD5 checksums of the audio data so corruption can be detected, and it embeds seektable markers so players can jump to a time offset without decoding from the start. The container supports arbitrary metadata (cover art, lyrics, replay-gain tags).

FLAC’s fixed-predictor mode is a brilliant engineering compromise: store no coefficients at all, predict with simple differences (𝑥𝑛−𝑥𝑛−1, or second-differences), and rely on the Rice coder. This makes the encoder extremely fast and the decoder trivially simple — ideal for hardware with limited CPU.

Worked Example: Encoding a Ramp

Suppose four samples are: [1000,1004,1009,1015]. Using the first-order fixed predictor (predict = previous sample):

  • Predictions: [−,1000,1004,1009]
  • Residuals: [1000,4,5,6] (the first sample is always stored verbatim)

With a Rice parameter 𝑘=3 (so 𝑀=8):

  • 10008=125 remainder 0: unary 125 ones + zero = 126 bits. (Clearly a bad choice for the first sample; FLAC would use verbatim for it.)
  • 48=0 remainder 4: unary 0 ones + zero + binary 100 = 4 bits.
  • 58=0 remainder 5: 4 bits.
  • 68=0 remainder 6: 4 bits.

After the verbatim first sample, three residuals that each cost 4 bits instead of 16 bits (raw PCM) — a 4× saving on those samples. The LPC predictor made the data compressible.

PY Go Further · Python — Simulating FLAC's Fixed Predictor in Pythonskip if fluent

This snippet computes first-order residuals and zigzag-encodes them for Rice coding.

def fixed_predict_order1(samples: list[int]) -> list[int]:
    """First-order fixed predictor: residual[n] = x[n] - x[n-1]."""
    residuals = [samples[0]]  # store first sample verbatim
    for i in range(1, len(samples)):
        residuals.append(samples[i] - samples[i - 1])
    return residuals

def zigzag(n: int) -> int:
    """Map signed int to non-negative: 0,1,-1,2,-2,... -> 0,1,2,3,4,..."""
    return (2 * n) if n >= 0 else (-2 * n - 1)

def rice_length(value: int, k: int) -> int:
    """Number of bits to encode non-negative value with Rice(k)."""
    q = value >> k   # quotient
    return q + 1 + k  # unary(q) + separator + k remainder bits

samples = [1000, 1004, 1009, 1015]
residuals = fixed_predict_order1(samples)
print("Residuals:", residuals)  # [1000, 4, 5, 6]

# Find the best Rice k for residuals[1:] (skip verbatim first sample)
tail = [zigzag(r) for r in residuals[1:]]
best_k = min(range(0, 8), key=lambda k: sum(rice_length(v, k) for v in tail))
total_bits = sum(rice_length(v, best_k) for v in tail)
print(f"Best k={best_k}, total bits for tail: {total_bits}")
# Best k=3, total bits for tail: 15  (vs 48 raw bits for 3 × 16-bit samples)

Run this and confirm you get Residuals: [1000, 4, 5, 6] and Best k=3 with 15 bits. (Because zigzag doubles each value, the residuals 4, 5, 6 become 8, 10, 12, so the best Rice parameter lands at k=3, not k=2.) That is 15 bits to carry three samples that would otherwise need 48 — roughly a 3× saving on this tiny ramp. The >> in value >> k is a bit-shift: value >> k is the same as value // (2**k), but faster.

ALAC — Apple’s Lossless Codec

ALAC (Apple Lossless Audio Codec) was introduced by Apple on April 28, 2004, alongside QuickTime 6.5.1, and was baked into iTunes 4.5. For seven years it was proprietary and undocumented — Apple never published a format spec, and its adoption outside Apple hardware was limited. On October 27, 2011, Apple released the complete source code and format specification under the Apache License 2.0, making ALAC royalty-free.

The 2011 open-source release was partly forced by practicality: the codec had already been reverse-engineered. In March 2005, David Hammerton published a clean-room ALAC decoder written in C, based purely on reverse engineering — a technically impressive feat that made proprietary obscurity increasingly pointless.

Technically, ALAC is close to FLAC in its approach: per-frame LPC analysis, residual coding. The key difference is the container: ALAC wraps its compressed frames inside the MP4 container (also called M4A when audio-only), using the ISO base media file format. This was deliberate — MP4 was already the standard container for iTunes. It meant that ALAC files could be embedded in the same infrastructure as AAC files, using the same metadata, artwork, and chapter-marker features. The tradeoff is that MP4 adds overhead and is somewhat less seekable than FLAC’s native container.

ALAC is the lossless format of choice for Apple Music’s lossless tier (launched June 2021). As of 2026, Apple Music streams ALAC at 16-bit/44.1 kHz (CD-quality) and up to 24-bit/192 kHz (high-resolution), delivered to iPhones, iPads, and Macs natively.

WavPack — the Hybrid Codec

WavPack was created by David Bryant and first released in mid-1998 — predating FLAC by three years. As of 2025, version 5.8 is current, with multicore encoding, WebAssembly decoding, and support for DSD (Direct Stream Digital) audio used in SACD discs.

WavPack’s signature feature, found in no other lossless codec, is its hybrid mode. In hybrid mode the encoder creates two output files:

  • A .wv file: a lossy-but-high-quality compressed stream at a user-specified bitrate (e.g. 256 kbps). This file sounds excellent and is much smaller than lossless.
  • A .wvc “correction” file: the exact residual needed to reconstruct the original losslessly. This file is typically 100–200 kbps.

When only the .wv file is available (e.g. streamed over a slow connection), you get lossy playback. When both are combined, you get perfect lossless reconstruction. This two-tier design was ahead of its time — a form of scalable coding that the broadcast world spent years trying to engineer into more complex codecs.

Aside. WavPack is one of the few audio codecs to natively handle 32-bit floating-point PCM, the native format of professional digital-audio-workstation (DAW) projects. While FLAC supports 32-bit integer, FLAC’s predictor is not designed for floats and achieves poor compression. WavPack uses a separate floating-point coding path that preserves all mantissa and exponent bits losslessly.

Monkey’s Audio — Maximum Compression at a Cost

Monkey’s Audio, created by Matthew T. Ashland and first released in 2000, chases the highest possible compression ratio above all other goals. Its file extension is .ape. At the “Insane” compression level, APE files are typically 5–8 % smaller than FLAC at its maximum setting — a meaningful margin when storing thousands of albums.

The price is computational cost. APE’s decoder is significantly more complex than FLAC’s. At high compression levels, the codec uses high-order predictors and a more expensive entropy coder. Many embedded players (portable music players, car stereos) struggle with the CPU load of decoding APE at maximum settings. Early iPod models refused to play APE at all. FLAC’s decoder, by contrast, was designed to run in real time on hardware from the early 2000s.

Monkey’s Audio remains popular in certain audiophile communities, particularly in China where .ape files have historically circulated widely. But for most practical purposes — hardware compatibility, streaming, long-term archival — FLAC has won.

Myth.Monkey’s Audio achieves much better compression than FLAC because it uses a fundamentally superior algorithm. Reality. The compression gap is real but modest (5–8 % at the extremes). The difference is that APE uses higher-order, more expensive models. FLAC’s designers deliberately capped complexity to ensure fast real-time decoding on constrained hardware. “Better” depends on your goal: if decode speed matters, FLAC is better; if file size is everything and CPU is free, APE is better.

Speech Coding — a Different Philosophy

Everything above stays strictly lossless. Speech codecs take the opposite extreme. A phone call over 4G (AMR-WB) might use 12.65 kbps — compared to CD audio’s 1,411 kbps. That is a compression ratio of over 100:1. No perceptual codec designed for music comes close to this.

How? By not compressing audio at all. Instead, speech codecs compress a model of the vocal tract and a description of what was said into it.

The Vocoder Idea

The vocoder (voice coder) is the conceptual grandparent of all speech codecs. It was invented by Homer Dudley at Bell Laboratories in 1936 and publicly demonstrated at the New York World’s Fair in 1939, where it ran on physical electrical circuits and delighted audiences by making a machine “speak.”

The key insight is this: human speech is produced by a two-part process. The source is either a buzzing (voiced sounds like vowels, where the vocal cords vibrate with a measurable pitch) or broadband noise (unvoiced sounds like “s” or “f”). The filter is the vocal tract — the throat, mouth, and nose — which acts as a resonating tube that shapes the source into the characteristic sounds of vowels and consonants.

Definition — Vocoder. A vocoder encodes speech by transmitting filter parameters (how the vocal tract is shaped) and source parameters (voiced/unvoiced, pitch frequency, loudness) separately. The decoder reconstructs the speech by running the source signal through a filter with the received parameters. The waveform at the decoder is entirely synthesized — it is not a recording of the original.

This model is powerful because the amount of information in the filter and source parameters is far smaller than the information in the waveform. A vocal-tract filter can be described by 10–16 numbers per 20 ms frame. A pitch period (for voiced speech) is a single number. A voiced/unvoiced flag is one bit. The resulting bitrate is staggeringly low — but the synthesized speech sounds robotic, because the model does not capture all the fine detail of the original waveform.

LPC-10 — the Government Vocoder

The first widely deployed LPC codec was LPC-10, standardized by the US Department of Defense as Federal Standard 1015 (FS-1015) and NATO STANAG 4198. It runs at exactly 2,400 bits per second — about 1.7 % of the bandwidth of a telephone-quality audio stream. The first real-time 2,400 bps LPC implementation was demonstrated at MIT Lincoln Laboratory in 1973.

LPC-10 analyzes each 22.5 ms frame of speech. For each frame it transmits:

  • LPC coefficients (order 10): describing the vocal tract filter.
  • Voiced/Unvoiced (V/UV) flag: one bit.
  • Pitch period (for voiced frames): integer in samples.
  • Gain: overall amplitude.

At 2,400 bps, the quality is distinctly “synthetic” — intelligible but clearly artificial. Military communications accepted this because 2,400 bps could be encrypted and transmitted over secure satellite links that full telephone audio could not use. LPC-10 remained in use until superseded by MELP (Mixed-Excitation Linear Prediction) in 1997.

CELP — Code-Excited Linear Prediction

The breakthrough that made speech codecs practical for commercial telephony was CELP, proposed by Manfred R. Schroeder and Bishnu S. Atal of Bell Labs in their landmark 1985 IEEE ICASSP paper.

The problem with LPC-10 was the source model. Using only voiced/unvoiced flags and a single pitch estimate loses too much of the richness of real speech — it sounds robotic. Schroeder and Atal’s insight: instead of a simplified source model, use a codebook of possible excitation signals, and transmit the index of whichever entry best explains the current residual. The codebook entry, scaled by a gain, is the excitation signal for that frame.

The name reflects this: Code-Excited Linear Prediction. The excitation is determined by a codebook index (a “code”), not a simple voiced/unvoiced rule.

Schroeder and Atal’s original 1983 simulations required 150 seconds of Cray-1 time to encode 1 second of speech. Within a decade, clever algebraic tricks and Moore’s Law made it fit on a chip. The computational journey from “requires a supercomputer” to “runs on a dumb 2G handset” is one of the most dramatic engineering compressions in the field.

How CELP Works: Analysis by Synthesis

CELP’s encoder uses a technique called analysis by synthesis. Rather than analyzing the input directly (as LPC-10 does), it tries different candidate excitation signals, synthesizes the speech each would produce using the LPC filter, and picks the one closest to the original. The loop works like this:

  1. For each 5–20 ms subframe of input speech: a. Compute current LPC coefficients for the vocal-tract filter. b. Search through the codebook for the entry 𝑐𝑖 such that the synthesized signal 𝑥̂=𝐻𝑐𝑖 (where 𝐻 is the LPC synthesis filter) minimizes a perceptually weighted error criterion (not plain mean squared error, but weighted to emphasize formant regions). c. Transmit the index 𝑖 and the gain.
  2. The decoder reconstructs speech by filtering the codebook entry through the LPC filter.

The perceptual weighting filter is the crucial innovation: it weights the error in the frequency domain so that noise is allowed to be larger where the signal is louder (at formants), and must be smaller in the quiet between-formant regions. This mimics masking. Crucially, it makes the quality of CELP speech far exceed what a raw MSE criterion would suggest.

CELP introduced a second codebook — the adaptive codebook — containing previous excitation vectors. This handles the long-term periodicity of voiced speech (the pitch pulse repeats every pitch period). The adaptive codebook search finds the best “pitch lag” and gain, and the fixed codebook refines the residual. Two-stage codebook + LPC filter is the architecture all modern speech codecs inherit.

ACELP — Making CELP Run on a Phone

The original CELP fixed codebook has thousands of entries, and searching it exhaustively is too slow for real-time use on hardware of the 1990s. Algebraic CELP (ACELP) solves this by replacing the arbitrary stored codebook with an algebraically structured codebook.

In an algebraic codebook, each entry is specified not by storing a whole vector but by specifying a small number of pulse positions and signs. For example, “two pulses: one positive pulse at position 3, one negative pulse at position 17” generates one codebook vector. Because the structure is purely positional, the search can be decomposed — instead of testing 213=8,192 entries, you search pulse positions independently, collapsing the search from exponential to polynomial.

Definition — Algebraic Codebook. An algebraic codebook represents each excitation vector as a sparse combination of unit pulses placed at specific positions. The “code” transmitted is a set of pulse positions and their signs, not an index into a stored table. This reduces codebook storage from many kilobytes to a compact description, and allows the search algorithm to decompose into fast sequential sub-searches.

ACELP was introduced in the mid-1990s and became the workhorse of digital telephony. The G.729 codec (8 kbps, 1996) and G.723.1 (5.3/6.3 kbps, 1995) standardized ACELP for the public telephone network. Then came the mobile world.

AMR — the Adaptive Multirate Codec

AMR-NB (Adaptive Multi-Rate Narrowband) was adopted by 3GPP in 1998 and became the standard speech codec for GSM and UMTS (3G) mobile networks worldwide. Its defining feature is its multirate design: it can switch between eight bitrate modes (4.75, 5.15, 5.90, 6.70, 7.40, 7.95, 10.2, 12.2 kbps) in real time, choosing the rate that fits current radio conditions. When the channel is good, it uses 12.2 kbps for maximum quality. When interference increases, it drops to 4.75 kbps to maintain the connection — better intelligible low-quality speech than dropped calls.

AMR-NB covers narrowband speech: 300–3,400 Hz, the classic telephone frequency range.

AMR-WB (Adaptive Multi-Rate Wideband), also known as G.722.2, was selected by 3GPP in December 2000 and standardized in January 2002. It extends the audio bandwidth to 50–7,000 Hz — a substantial improvement in naturalness and speaker recognition. The difference between narrowband and wideband is immediately audible: narrowband speech sounds “telephone-y,” while wideband sounds close to face-to-face conversation. AMR-WB supports nine modes ranging from 6.60 to 23.85 kbps.

Both AMR-NB and AMR-WB use ACELP under the hood.

Why AMR Changed the World

Before AMR, mobile phones used a single fixed codec: GSM-FR (Full Rate) at 13 kbps. Quality was fixed regardless of channel conditions. When the signal weakened, calls dropped rather than gracefully degrading. AMR’s rate-switching was a fundamental architectural improvement: it turned a binary “connected/disconnected” into a continuous quality spectrum. This is the same insight that drives adaptive bitrate (ABR) streaming in video (Chapter 55) — match your bitrate to what the channel can support.

Historical note The AMR-WB codec algorithm was developed primarily at Nokia Research Center (Finland) and Ericsson Research (Sweden), submitted to 3GPP as the “VOICEAGE” candidate in 1999–2000. Its inventor, Vilmos Varsa (Nokia), along with Ericsson’s team, won the 3GPP selection in December 2000. AMR-WB is now also ITU-T G.722.2 — the first time the same codec held both a 3GPP standard number and an ITU-T recommendation, reflecting the global unification of telephony standards in the 2000s.

EVS — Enhanced Voice Services

By the mid-2010s, mobile networks were transitioning from circuit-switched voice (2G/3G) to VoLTE (Voice over LTE) — voice carried as IP packets over 4G. This created both an opportunity and a need for a better codec. EVS is the result.

EVS (Enhanced Voice Services) was standardized by 3GPP in December 2014 as Release 12 (TS 26.441–26.448). It is a major step beyond AMR-WB in several respects:

  • Bandwidth: EVS supports narrowband (up to 4 kHz), wideband (up to 8 kHz), super-wideband (up to 14 kHz), and full-band (up to 20 kHz) audio. Full-band covers the entire audible range of typical speech, plus music.
  • Bitrates: 5.9 to 128 kbps across all modes. At the low end, EVS is a speech-only codec; at the high end (64–128 kbps full-band), it delivers near-transparent quality for music.
  • Mixed content: At higher bitrates EVS uses an MDCT-based coding layer for music and background audio, alongside ACELP for speech. It switches between them frame by frame.
  • AMR-WB compatibility: EVS includes an AMR-WB-compatible interoperability mode so calls can hand off between EVS and AMR-WB networks seamlessly.
  • Error concealment: Designed for LTE packet loss, with sophisticated packet loss concealment algorithms.

Neural Speech Codecs — the Horizon

Chapter 49 introduced LPCNet as part of the Opus 1.5 discussion (Opus uses a neural network for packet-loss concealment). It is worth pausing here to note why neural methods attack speech coding so naturally.

The source-filter model that underlies all CELP codecs is an approximation. It assumes the vocal tract is a linear filter and the excitation is either periodic or noisy — but real voices have subtle correlations, vibrato, breathiness, and hundreds of micro-textures that algebraic codebooks miss. A neural network trained on thousands of hours of speech can learn to predict these textures from the LPC residual, essentially acting as a learned codebook that is far denser than any algebraic one.

LPCNet (Valin and Skoglund, 2019) hybridizes classic CELP and deep learning: the decoder uses LPC coefficients (transmitted classically) plus a WaveRNN-style neural network to synthesize the waveform sample-by-sample. At 1.6 kbps it produces speech quality that beats CELP at much higher bitrates.

Google’s Lyra (2021) and the subsequent Lyra v2 (2022) take this further: at 3 kbps, Lyra v2 (using a generative model called SoundStream’s decoder) produces speech that listeners prefer to AMR-WB at 9 kbps in blind tests.

These codecs will not fully replace CELP-based standards in the near future — standards processes are slow, and neural decoders are power-hungry on cheap hardware. But they demonstrate that the floor of intelligible speech quality at ultra-low bitrates is still moving downward. We revisit the machine-learning machinery in Chapter 56, and neural audio codecs as discrete tokenizers (SoundStream, EnCodec, DAC) get a full chapter of their own in Chapter 60.

Checkpoint: A FLAC file for a 3-minute classical piano piece is 28 MB. The raw 16-bit 44.1 kHz stereo PCM would be about 47 MB. Approximately what compression ratio did FLAC achieve? Is this surprising given what you know about lossless compression?
47 / 28 ≈ 1.68×, or about 40 % reduction. This is typical — piano has high spectral complexity and the LPC predictor cannot compress it as aggressively as, say, a quiet voice recording. Not surprising; lossless music compression is bounded by the true entropy of musical audio.
Checkpoint: A CELP codec must search a codebook of 512 entries for each of 4 subframes per frame. Each codebook entry has 40 samples. How many multiply-accumulate operations (a multiply-accumulate, or MAC, is one “multiply two numbers and add to a running total” — the basic step of filtering) does one frame’s codebook search require (ignoring the perceptual weighting filter)?
512 entries × 4 subframes × 40 multiply-accumulates = 81,920 MACs per frame. At 50 frames/second (20 ms frames), that is about 4 million MACs/second — achievable on a DSP of the late 1990s, though it required careful optimization.

Putting It Together: Why These Two Families Coexist

It might seem that FLAC and CELP are solving completely different problems, but they share a conceptual structure:

  • Both use linear prediction to model temporal structure in the audio.
  • Both transmit residuals (what the model got wrong).
  • Both use efficient codes for those residuals (Rice for lossless, codebooks for speech).

The key difference is what happens next. FLAC transmits every residual bit exactly — no approximation. CELP quantizes the residual heavily (with a codebook), discards everything the quantizer cannot represent, and reconstructs a close approximation — one that sounds good but is not the original waveform.

This is not a compromise in CELP’s case: the decoder synthesizes speech through the LPC filter. The waveform it produces is not a degraded version of the original — it is a synthesized speech signal that conveys the same linguistic content and much of the same voice character. At low enough bitrates, this synthesis approach produces better-sounding results than any waveform-preserving codec.

Key idea The difference between lossless and speech coding is not just “perfect vs. imperfect reconstruction.” It is a difference in what is being compressed: lossless codecs compress the waveform, while speech codecs compress a model of how the waveform was produced. Compressing the model is far more efficient — but only works when you know the model.
PY Go Further · Python — Illustrating the Vocoder Concept: LPC Analysis in 20 Linesskip if fluent

This simplified example computes LPC coefficients for a short speech-like signal using the autocorrelation method — the core step every speech codec performs every 20 ms.

import math

def autocorr(signal: list[float], lag: int) -> float:
    """Compute autocorrelation of signal at given lag."""
    n = len(signal)
    total = 0.0
    for i in range(n - lag):
        total += signal[i] * signal[i + lag]
    return total

def levinson(r: list[float]) -> list[float]:
    """Levinson-Durbin recursion: solve R*a = r for LPC coefficients.
    r[0] = autocorr at lag 0, r[1] at lag 1, etc.
    Returns coefficients a[1..p] (predictor weights)."""
    p = len(r) - 1
    a = [0.0] * (p + 1)
    err = r[0]
    for i in range(1, p + 1):
        # reflection coefficient k_i
        num = r[i] + sum(-a[j] * r[i - j] for j in range(1, i))
        k = num / err
        # update coefficients
        a_new = a[:]
        for j in range(1, i):
            a_new[j] = a[j] - k * a[i - j]
        a_new[i] = -k
        a = a_new
        err *= (1.0 - k * k)
    return a[1:]  # a[1..p]

# Simulate a voiced speech frame: 160 samples of a 100 Hz sine at 8000 Hz sample rate
N = 160
freq = 100.0
Fs = 8000.0
samples = [math.sin(2 * math.pi * freq * n / Fs) for n in range(N)]

order = 4
r = [autocorr(samples, lag) for lag in range(order + 1)]
coeffs = levinson(r)
print("LPC coefficients (order 4):", [f"{c:.4f}" for c in coeffs])
# For a pure sine wave, a 2nd-order predictor can predict it exactly.
# At order 4 you will see most energy in the first two coefficients.

How to read this. The levinson function implements the Yule-Walker solution in 𝑂(𝑝2) operations. The output coefficients describe a filter that models the “vocal tract” of this signal. Every 20 ms, a speech codec runs exactly this — on real speech — and transmits the resulting 8–16 numbers instead of the 160 raw samples.

A Note on Lossless Limits

We said FLAC achieves roughly 40–60 % reduction on music. But can we be more precise about the ceiling?

Claude Shannon’s source coding theorem (Chapter 19) says no lossless code can compress below the entropy rate of the source. For audio, the “source” includes all the statistical structure: within-frame correlations (which LPC exploits), across-frame correlations (which some codecs partially exploit through long-term prediction), stereo correlation (joint stereo coding, which FLAC supports via “mid-side” mode), and frequency-domain structure (which FLAC does not exploit, because doing so would complicate the predictor).

Modern research suggests the theoretical limit for lossless audio compression on typical music is around 30–35 % of raw size — about 2.9–3.3×. FLAC at default settings reaches  1.8×; at maximum settings  1.95×. So there is still a gap, but closing it would require far more complex models.

Lossless codecs deliberately leave this gap open in exchange for fast decoding and robustness. This is an engineering choice, not a failure of the math.

Historical note The history of lossless audio is quieter than the patent wars around MP3 and AAC, because lossless codecs have generally avoided patents. Josh Coalson released FLAC under a BSD licence from day one, explicitly to prevent the kind of royalty disputes that had embroiled the lossy world. Apple’s decision to open-source ALAC in 2011 (prompted partly by growing community pressure and the success of FLAC) was another signal that the industry recognized that patent-free lossless codecs benefit everyone — hardware makers, streaming services, and listeners alike. The December 2024 publication of RFC 9639 is the culmination: FLAC is now an IETF standard, as rigorous and permanent as the protocols underlying the internet itself.

Chapter takeaways

  • Lossless audio codecs (FLAC, ALAC, WavPack, Monkey’s Audio) all use the same core engine: linear prediction to generate small residuals, plus Rice/Golomb entropy coding for those residuals. The differences are in predictor design, container format, and compression-vs-speed tradeoffs.
  • FLAC is the open standard (RFC 9639, December 2024), released by Josh Coalson in 2001 under a BSD licence, with universal hardware support and  40–60 % size reduction on music.
  • ALAC lives in the Apple MP4 ecosystem; WavPack’s hybrid mode provides both a portable lossy file and a lossless correction file; Monkey’s Audio squeezes 5–8 % more at the cost of far slower decoding.
  • The lossless compression ceiling for music is approximately 1.8–2.1× for typical codecs, bounded by the true Shannon entropy of musical audio.
  • Speech codecs take a completely different approach: model the vocal tract as a source (buzzing or noise) plus a resonant filter, and transmit model parameters instead of the waveform.
  • LPC-10 (2,400 bps, 1973) was the first government-standard speech codec; CELP (Schroeder and Atal, Bell Labs, 1985) replaced its simplified source model with a codebook, dramatically improving naturalness.
  • ACELP reduced CELP’s codebook search from exponential to polynomial complexity by using algebraic (sparse pulse) codebooks, enabling real-time operation on mobile hardware.
  • AMR-NB (1998) and AMR-WB (2002) brought adaptive bitrate switching to mobile voice; EVS (2014) added super-wideband and full-band support, and is the current standard for VoLTE and 5G NR.
  • Neural vocoders such as LPCNet and Lyra now outperform classical CELP at ultra-low bitrates by learning the excitation distribution from data; Chapter 60 covers neural audio codecs in depth.

Exercise 50.2 ★ Explain in plain words why Rice codes are near-optimal for LPC residuals. What statistical property of the residuals makes them a good fit?
Solution 50.2LPC residuals follow an approximately Laplacian (two-sided exponential) distribution: most values cluster near zero, with the probability of value 𝑛 proportional to 𝜆|𝑛| for some 𝜆<1. Rice codes are optimal for geometric distributions, which are the discrete analogue of the exponential. By choosing the Rice parameter 𝑘 to match the typical magnitude of residuals in each block, the coder assigns short codes to common small values and longer codes to rare large values — matching the distribution and approaching the entropy limit.
Exercise 50.3 ★★ Write a Python function rice_encode(values: list[int], k: int) -> list[int] that takes a list of signed integers and returns a list of bit counts — the total bits needed to Rice-encode each value (use the zigzag mapping for negatives). Compute the total bits for values = [0, 1, -1, 3, -5, 10] at k = 2 and at k = 3. Which k is better?
Solution 50.3
def zigzag(n: int) -> int:
    return 2 * n if n >= 0 else -2 * n - 1

def rice_bits(values: list[int], k: int) -> int:
    total = 0
    for v in values:
        u = zigzag(v)
        q = u >> k
        total += q + 1 + k  # unary(q) + stop bit + k remainder bits
    return total

vals = [0, 1, -1, 3, -5, 10]
print(rice_bits(vals, 2))  # k=2: total bits
print(rice_bits(vals, 3))  # k=3: total bits

k=2 gives: zigzag → [0,2,1,6,9,20]; quotients 𝑞=𝑢≫2 are [0,0,0,1,2,5]; bits per value are 𝑞+1+2: (0+1+2)+(0+1+2)+(0+1+2)+(1+1+2)+(2+1+2)+(5+1+2) = 3+3+3+4+5+8 = 26. k=3 gives: same zigzag; quotients 𝑞=𝑢≫3 are [0,0,0,0,1,2]; bits per value are 𝑞+1+3: (0+1+3)+(0+1+3)+(0+1+3)+(0+1+3)+(1+1+3)+(2+1+3)

4+4+4+4+5+6 = 27.

k=2 is better (26 < 27 bits) for this distribution of values.

Exercise 50.5 ★★ Compare FLAC and ALAC from a format-design perspective. Why did Apple choose to wrap ALAC inside an MP4 container? What is lost and what is gained by this choice?
Solution 50.5Apple chose MP4 because iTunes already used MP4/M4A for AAC, and sharing the container format means the same tools, libraries, metadata APIs, and hardware parsers work for both lossy (AAC) and lossless (ALAC) content. Solution 50.6(a) AMR-NB: 12.2 kbps × 30 s / 8 = 45.75 kB compressed. Raw PCM: 8,000 × 2 × 30 = 480,000 bytes = 468 kB.   • Valin, J.-M. and Skoglund, J. (2019). LPCNet: Improving Neural Speech Synthesis Through Linear Prediction. arXiv:1810.11846. — The paper that showed classical LPC coefficients combined with a neural decoder can beat CELP at 1.6 kbps.

  • Brandenburg, K. (1999). MP3 and AAC Explained. AES 17th International Conference. — Karlheinz Brandenburg’s own account of the development of MP3 and AAC, valuable context for how the perceptual coding paradigm was established.

  • WavPack Wikipedia article. — Covers the hybrid mode in detail and the evolution through version 5.x.

Coming up next We have now covered the full landscape of audio compression: from psychoacoustics and lossy perceptual codecs (Chapters 46–49) to lossless and speech coding (this chapter). Audio, however, is only half the story of the media pipeline that delivers most of the world’s compressed bits. The vast majority of data flowing across the internet is video: moving images at 24–120 frames per second, each frame as complex as the JPEG images of Chapter 42 — but with the added dimension of time. Chapter 51 introduces the idea that makes video compression possible at all: motion compensation. If two consecutive video frames are similar, we can describe the second frame as a slightly shifted version of the first, plus a small correction. The correction is far cheaper to code than a new frame. That is the insight behind every video codec from H.261 (1988) to AV1 (2018) to the learned neural video codecs of Chapter 59.
